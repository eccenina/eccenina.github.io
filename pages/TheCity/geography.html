Surface Drainage - The Mestizo River

Vigan was once totally encircled by water that jutted out from the shore like a triangle island, according to a chart from the sixteenth century. Geological observation supported this finding because it could be determined that the water bodies in the vicinity of Vigan were the primary channels that were then running in a westerly direction and branching as the river sliced the Vigan ridge.

According to 16th century maps, Vigan was once surrounded by water, forming a triangular island from the mainland. This was confirmed by geological observations showing that the waters around Vigan were a major watercourse that then diverged into rivers that flowed westward and divided the Vigan ridge.

Currently, the Mestizo River flows only through Vigan. There are very few fish and they are becoming scarce.

A brief survey was conducted to collect preliminary geological data needed for the Mestizo River restoration and development plan. Vigan has a climate that is classified as having the first climate type in the Philippines, which has two distinct seasons. The dry season often starts in November and lasts through April, peaking in the hot months of April and May. June through October are the wettest months, with August being the wettest month. The greatest recorded rainfall is 693.3 mm, and the average annual rainfall is 190.683 mm. 26 °C is the average temperature. The average high and low temperatures are 30.9 °C and 21.1 °C, respectively. Every year, 7–10 typhoons pass through Vigan. The relative humidity is 81% on average.

Hydrogeology

The Vigan Banthai Plain is clearly free of collapse and pointing, except for the eastern and southern extremities of the plain. A north-south fault runs through the Pre-Quaternary rocks on the eastern margin. The southern extremity of the plain is bounded by another tectonic line running northwest and southeast from Santa to Narvakan and Sutha, and fault lines run from Sinait to Bantai and Santa. Maria.

The city's sedimentary and metamorphic rocks include alluvium, fluviatille, lacuatine, pludal, and beach deposits, which include: stools, coral reefs, and beach rock The majority of these can be found in Vigan's coastal areas. The type of clay known locally as "burnay" that is utilized in the production of earthen jars is an important non-metallic mineral resource that can be found in Vigan. This kind of clay is used to make a variety of sizes and uses for earthenware. The mineral, which is referred to as Bantog Clay, serves as the primary building block for bricks. The original building materials for the Vigan's historic Hispanic homes were these bricks.
